# Company Targeting for the Bird-Strike Composite Benchmark

## Purpose
Use the same technical project, but change the framing depending on the company. Recruiters and hiring managers do not all care about the same structural story.

This document maps your bird-to-composite plate benchmark to five target-company contexts:

- Anduril
- Joby
- Archer
- Boom
- SpaceX

## Positioning rule
Do not claim the project solves the exact problem of a given company unless the link is direct and defensible.

Instead, position it as evidence that you can:

- build explicit dynamic FEA workflows,
- analyze composite damage and residual capability,
- encode manufacturing variation in a simulation-driven process,
- and communicate results in a certification-aware engineering language.

That is credible across all five companies.

## How to tailor the same project

### 1. Anduril
#### What they are likely to care about
- rapid design iteration,
- deployable aerospace hardware,
- structural margin under uncertain operational loading,
- manufacturability and scale,
- practical analysis turnaround instead of only thesis-level fidelity.

#### Best project framing
Frame the project as a **fast-turn, decision-oriented impact and damage-tolerance benchmark** for composite airframe structures in attritable or mission-driven aerospace systems.

#### What to emphasize
- you can build a repeatable analysis workflow, not just a one-off model,
- you understand residual capability after impact,
- you can parameterize manufacturing defects and identify which ones actually move the answer,
- you think in terms of engineering screening and risk reduction.

#### Good outreach language
> I built a composite impact benchmark that focuses on penetration resistance, damage footprint, and post-impact residual capability under manufacturing variability. The part I think is most relevant to Anduril is the workflow discipline: it is structured to support quick design trades rather than only one-off research simulations.

#### What not to overclaim
Do not overfocus on FAA certification language here. Anduril will care more about analysis utility, robustness, and mission relevance than transport-category compliance framing.

### 2. Joby
#### What they are likely to care about
- FAA certification credibility,
- composite primary structure behavior,
- damage tolerance,
- manufacturability,
- test-analysis correlation.

#### Best project framing
Frame the project as a **certification-aware composite damage and residual-strength study** that shows you understand how impact damage must be translated into structural disposition decisions.

#### What to emphasize
- certification-aware assumptions,
- residual stiffness or residual load-carrying capability after impact,
- careful defect parameterization,
- validation logic and model credibility checks,
- communication in a way a DER or certification structures team would respect.

#### Good outreach language
> I designed the benchmark around certification-style questions rather than only impact visuals: no-penetration, damage extent, and residual structural capability. I also treated manufacturing defects as explicit variables, because as-designed and as-built composite structures are not the same.

#### What not to overclaim
Do not imply this is a direct Part 23/27/29 or special-condition compliance model unless it actually maps to a specific requirement and test article.

### 3. Archer
#### What they are likely to care about
- eVTOL structure certification,
- manufacturable composite structures,
- lightweight design with realistic damage constraints,
- analysis tied to design and test execution.

#### Best project framing
Frame the project as a **composite structures damage-tolerance workflow** that bridges analysis, manufacturing realism, and design decision support.

#### What to emphasize
- your composites background is directly relevant,
- you care about how defects alter the margin, not just nominal response,
- you can move from impact event to residual capability metric,
- you know how to keep the model simple enough to be useful.

#### Good outreach language
> The part of the work I would highlight for Archer is not just the explicit impact simulation. It is the link between process-driven defect surrogates, progressive damage, and a residual-capability metric that could inform structural design decisions.

#### What not to overclaim
Avoid positioning the work as if bird strike is the dominant structural challenge for Archer. The stronger signal is that you can handle certification-relevant composite damage problems rigorously.

### 4. Boom
#### What they are likely to care about
- high-performance airframe structures,
- certification in a difficult regime,
- lightweight structures that still tolerate off-nominal events,
- evidence of practical analysis depth.

#### Best project framing
Frame the project as a **high-rate composite impact and residual-damage benchmark** demonstrating explicit-dynamics capability plus engineering judgment around failure interpretation.

#### What to emphasize
- transient nonlinear explicit analysis,
- composites failure interpretation,
- energy accounting and numerical credibility,
- structured validation rather than black-box simulation.

#### Good outreach language
> I used the project to demonstrate more than explicit impact modeling. The useful part is showing how to separate physically meaningful damage trends from numerical artifacts and then convert the result into a residual-structure decision metric.

#### What not to overclaim
Do not imply the panel benchmark directly represents a supersonic transport structure. It is better to say it demonstrates transferable analysis capability in impact, failure, and post-damage assessment.

### 5. SpaceX
#### What they are likely to care about
- extreme development velocity,
- test-heavy hardware iteration,
- structural analysis tightly coupled to manufacturing,
- ability to work across nonlinear behavior and failure.

#### Best project framing
Frame the project as a **high-rate structural damage benchmark that converts nonlinear simulation into actionable design insight under manufacturing variability**.

#### What to emphasize
- you can handle nonlinear transient events,
- you can reduce a complex model to a few decision metrics,
- you are comfortable with imperfect hardware and as-built variation,
- you are not dependent on polished certification narratives to do useful structural work.

#### Good outreach language
> What I think transfers best to SpaceX is the engineering pattern: define a compact benchmark, quantify sensitivity to manufacturing variability, and reduce a nonlinear failure event into a decision-oriented result set that can inform hardware iteration quickly.

#### What not to overclaim
Do not pitch this as a direct launch-vehicle impact problem. The transferable part is the speed and rigor of your structural analysis workflow under nonlinear damage.
